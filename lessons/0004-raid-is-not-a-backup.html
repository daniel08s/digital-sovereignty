RAID is not a backup

Your mission includes photos and documents on hardware you control. Before you buy disks or pick TrueNAS, you need one mental model that stops expensive mistakes: redundancy keeps a box online when a drive dies; a backup is what saves you when the box — or everything in the house — is gone.

~12 min Concept Win: apply 3-2-1 to your own data, and know what ZFS uniquely buys you

Two jobs people confuse

Redundancy (RAID / ZFS pool)Backup (3-2-1)
Survives a dead drive?YesYes (if you have copies)
Survives "rm -rf" / bad sync?No — deletes hit every diskYes — restore from an older copy
Survives ransomware?No — encrypts the whole poolYes — if one copy is offline/off-site
Survives fire / theft?No — one physical boxYes — the off-site copy

A mirrored NAS with six healthy drives and no separate copy still has one copy of your data. That is not sovereignty — that is a single point of failure with a fancy filesystem.

The sentence to memorise

RAID (and a ZFS pool) is redundancy for uptime. Backups are copies for recovery. You want both. Official NAS docs say the same thing: redundancy is not a substitute for a backup strategy (TrueNAS ZFS Primer).

The 3-2-1 rule

CISA recommends this baseline (originally popularised by photographer Peter Krogh):

3 copies of important data
primary + at least two more
2 different media types
e.g. NAS disks + USB/external or cloud object storage
1 copy off-site
survives house fire, flood, theft

A concrete shape for your mission

You do not need to buy anything yet — just picture the slots:

CopyWhereJob
1 — primaryHome NAS (ZFS pool)Day-to-day photos, docs, media library
2 — local backupSecond machine, or external drive you unplugFast restore; survives NAS death / oops-delete
3 — off-siteCloud backup, or a drive kept elsewhereSurvives the house going wrong

ZFS snapshots on the NAS are brilliant for "I deleted that yesterday" — but they live on the same pool. They count toward recovery convenience, not toward the off-site "1" until you replicate them somewhere else.

Test restores, or you don't have a backup

CISA also stresses: secure backups (encryption, offline where it matters) and test that you can restore. An untested backup is a hypothesis. For home: once a quarter, restore one random folder from the off-site copy and open a few files.

Why ZFS, not "just RAID"

Traditional RAID answers: "what if a drive dies?" It does not answer: "what if a drive quietly corrupts a photo while claiming it's fine?" That failure mode is bit rot.

ZFS (via TrueNAS / OpenZFS) checksums every block on write and verifies on read. If a block is wrong and redundancy still has a good copy, ZFS self-heals it. Periodic scrubs read the whole pool so rarely-touched files get checked too (TrueNAS ZFS Primer).

For irreplaceable family photos and documents — the sovereignty case — that integrity story is why this course leans ZFS, not bare mdadm RAID or a single-disk USB drive as the primary store.

What's next (not this lesson)

Pool layouts (mirror vs RAIDZ2), how many drives, and hardware choices come after this mental model. Buying disks before you know "RAID ≠ backup" and "plan the vdev before you create the pool" is how people paint themselves into a corner.
