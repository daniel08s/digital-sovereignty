How two routers chain together

You understood that a DMZ sends inbound traffic from the ISP router to the Flint 2. This lesson answers the reverse: what is the Flint 2's WAN, does it copy the ISP router's IP, and why does outbound traffic just work with no rule to configure?

~7 min Concepts-first Win: explain both directions of traffic

Your Flint 2 is "just another device" on the ISP router

Physically, a cable runs from a LAN port on the ISP router to the WAN port on the Flint 2. From the ISP router's point of view, the Flint 2 is nothing special — it plugged in and got a private address, exactly like your laptop would. But hidden behind that one device is a whole second network. You end up with two stacked private subnets:

Same subnet, different address — never identical

The Flint 2's WAN is 192.168.1.2; the ISP router is 192.168.1.1. Same neighbourhood (192.168.1.x), different houses. Giving them the same address would be an IP conflict and break the network. You don't copy the ISP router's IP — you sit next to it.

Make that WAN IP stable (so the DMZ target never moves)

The goal isn't to match the ISP router — it's to make the Flint 2's WAN IP predictable, so "DMZ → 192.168.1.2" always hits it. Two equivalent ways:

  • DHCP reservation on the ISP router: "always give the Flint 2's MAC the address 192.168.1.2."
  • Static WAN on the Flint 2: set WAN mode = static, IP 192.168.1.2, gateway 192.168.1.1.

The "reverse": why outbound needs no rule

Here's the key idea you were missing: NAT is stateful. Every time a device makes an outbound connection, the router writes a temporary note — "192.168.10.42 opened a chat with youtube.com." When the reply returns, the router matches it to that note and lets it back in automatically. End the conversation and the note is deleted. There is no "reverse DMZ" to configure.

Follow one packet from your NAS out to the internet — notice it's a chain of gateways, each router pointing "up" to the next:

StepWhoWhat happens
1NAS .10.42Sends packet to its gateway, the Flint 2 (192.168.10.1)
2Flint 2NAT: rewrites source to WAN 192.168.1.2, notes it, forwards to its gateway 192.168.1.1
3ISP routerNAT again: rewrites source to your public IP, notes it, sends to the internet
4the replyPublic IP → ISP router checks notes → 192.168.1.2 → Flint 2 checks notes → NAS. Automatic.

The rule that ties it all together

You only need a DMZ or port-forward for connections that start from the outside — like your phone at a café reaching your WireGuard server. The router has no note for those, so it drops them by default (which is what protects you). Outbound connections and their replies always find their way back on their own.

That's why WireGuard is so tidy: you open one door (ISP DMZ → Flint 2). Once the tunnel is up, your phone acts like it lives on 192.168.10.x, so reaching the NAS or Jellyfin uses the ordinary automatic path — no per-service holes.
