Reading it, layer by layer

1. One public address, many private ones

Your ISP gives your home a single public IP — one street address for the whole building. Inside, every device gets its own private IP from the reserved RFC1918 ranges (yours are the 192.168.x.x family). Private addresses can't be reached from the internet directly — that's a feature, not a limitation.

2. NAT: how one address serves many devices

When your laptop asks for a web page, the router runs NAT: it swaps your laptop's private "from" address for the public one, notes in a ledger who asked, and sends it out. The reply comes back to the public IP, the router checks its ledger, and hands it to your laptop. Every home router does this. It's why "what's my IP" (public) differs from your device's 192.168.… (private).

3. The subnet: 192.168.10.0/24

The /24 means the first three numbers are the neighbourhood and the last is the house. So everything from 192.168.10.1 to 192.168.10.254 lives on the same street and can talk directly; anything else must go through the router (the gateway, usually 192.168.10.1). Choosing .10. instead of the default .1. or .0. is a small nicety: it avoids clashes when you later connect two networks over a VPN.

4. DHCP: addresses handed out automatically

DHCP is the service that gives each device an address as it joins. Your plan says the Flint 2 is the only DHCP server — exactly right. Two DHCP servers on one network fight over who assigns what and cause chaos. (For your NAS and WireGuard box you'll later want a reservation so their address never moves.)

The "aha": double NAT, and why the DMZ line exists

You have two routers doing NAT in series — the ISP router and the Flint 2. That's double NAT. Browsing still works, but reaching into your network from outside (for your WireGuard server) becomes a two-lock door: you'd have to open a path on both routers.

Your fix: reserve 192.168.1.2 for the Flint 2 on the ISP router, then set that router's DMZ to 192.168.1.2. "DMZ → this IP" means forward all inbound traffic to the Flint 2. The ISP box becomes a dumb pipe; your Flint 2 becomes the single, real border you control. One lock instead of two.
