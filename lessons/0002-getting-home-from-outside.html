Getting home from outside

You can reach the internet from home automatically. Now for the reverse: reaching home from a café or cellular data — securely, without flinging your services open to the internet. This lesson builds a WireGuard server on the Flint 2, opens exactly one door for it, and tests it from outside.

~15 min Build lesson Win: connect to your LAN from anywhere

Recap: why you only need one door

From Lesson 1a: outbound connections and their replies flow back automatically, because NAT is stateful. Only connections started from the outside need a deliberate opening. A VPN turns "reach my dozen home services from outside" into a single problem: open one door for the VPN, and once the tunnel is up your phone behaves as if it's sitting on 192.168.10.x — every service reachable by the ordinary automatic path, nothing else exposed.

Step 0 — the go/no-go check: do you have a real public IP?

Inbound access only works if your ISP gives you a genuine public IP. If you're behind CGNAT (carrier-grade NAT), unsolicited inbound traffic dies at the ISP's equipment before it ever reaches you — no port-forward can fix that.

The check: find your ISP router's WAN IP, then compare it to what ipconfig.io (or "what is my IP") shows. Match = real public IP, continue. Different, or WAN IP in 100.64.x.x–100.127.x.x or a private range → CGNAT → use Tailscale instead (it punches through CGNAT automatically).

Choosing the tool: WireGuard vs Tailscale

Both use the same fast, modern WireGuard protocol for the actual tunnel. The difference is who does the setup work:

Raw WireGuard (on the Flint 2)Tailscale
SetupYou open one port + manage keysAutomatic, ~5 min
Needs a public IP?Yes (or IPv6)No — traverses CGNAT
Third-party dependencyNone — you own it allTailscale's coordination server
Best when…You have a public IP & want full controlBehind CGNAT, or want zero fuss

Since your CGNAT check passed and the Flint 2 has a built-in WireGuard server, raw WireGuard is the clean, dependency-free choice here. (If your ISP ever moves you to CGNAT, Tailscale is the drop-in fallback.)

The build

1. Enable DDNS (a stable name for a moving IP)

Your public IP can change over time. Dynamic DNS gives you a hostname that always tracks it. On the Flint 2: APPLICATIONS → Dynamic DNS, toggle on, accept terms, Apply. Note the hostname (e.g. something.glddns.com) — your client will use this as its endpoint. Allow ~10 minutes to propagate.

2. Start the WireGuard server

VPN → WireGuard Server → Start. Sensible defaults, and why:

SettingValueWhy
Listen port51820 (UDP)Must match the port you'll forward. Keep them identical.
IPv4 address10.1.0.1/24The tunnel subnet — a virtual network living only inside the VPN. Leave it: it mustn't overlap your LAN (192.168.10.0/24), the ISP subnet, or the network you connect from. 10.1.0.x avoids all three.
ObfuscationOffGL.iNet's obfuscation is AmneziaWG — the standard WireGuard app can't connect to it. Only enable if a network actively blocks WireGuard (and use the AmneziaWG client then).

3. Open exactly one door — port-forward, not DMZ

On the ISP router, add a single rule: UDP 51820 → 192.168.1.2 (the Flint 2's WAN). That's it.

Port-forward vs DMZ — pick one, pick port-forward

They're two ways to do the same job; using both just re-widens the hole. A port-forward opens one port and the ISP router keeps dropping everything else. A DMZ forwards all ports to the Flint 2, switching off the ISP router's filtering entirely.

Port-forward wins because it keeps two firewalls filtering (ISP router + Flint 2) instead of one, and shrinks your internet-facing attack surface to a single UDP port. Think of a lobby guard who only admits "room 51820" vs. removing the front door and hoping every apartment's lock holds. Keep DMZ off; reach for it only as a temporary debugging step.

4. Create a client profile

On the WireGuard Server, add a profile (one per device). Enable "use DDNS domain" so the endpoint is your glddns.com name, not a raw IP. The Flint 2 generates a QR code / .conf file — open the official WireGuard app on your phone and scan it.

Full tunnel vs split tunnel (you can change this anytime)

A client's AllowedIPs decides what routes through the tunnel:

  • Split tunnel — only home-LAN traffic goes through; normal browsing stays direct. Faster; good for "just reach my homelab."
  • Full tunnel (0.0.0.0/0) — all phone traffic routes through home, so you also get your home IP and (later) AdGuard ad-blocking while out. Uses more bandwidth.

Start with the default; revisit after the AdGuard lesson, where full-tunnel becomes "ad-blocking anywhere."

5. Test from a real external network

Turn off Wi-Fi on your phone (cellular only = genuinely outside), activate the tunnel, and try to reach the Flint 2 admin at 192.168.10.1 or your NAS. Debugging order if it fails: first prove the tunnel works using the Flint 2's local IP while on your home Wi-Fi, then switch the endpoint to the DDNS name and retest from cellular. Common culprits: DDNS not propagated yet, forwarding set to TCP instead of UDP, or the port-forward pointing at the wrong internal IP.
