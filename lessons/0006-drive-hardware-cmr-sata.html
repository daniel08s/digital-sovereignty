Drive hardware: CMR, zoned storage, SATA vs SAS

You know to aim for a RAIDZ2 vdev. Now the disks themselves. Two shopping questions decide whether that pool stays healthy under resilver stress: how the platters are written (CMR vs zoned/SMR), and which cable protocol you use (SATA vs SAS).

~12 min Concept Win: reject zoned/SMR for ZFS RAIDZ2; default to CMR SATA NAS drives

What “zoned storage” actually means

A zoned storage device divides the disk into zones. Inside a zone, writes must go in order (sequential) — like filling a notebook page top-to-bottom, not scribbling anywhere. That model powers host-managed SMR hard drives (ZBC/ZAC) and NVMe Zoned Namespaces (ZNS).

Why vendors like it: denser platters or cheaper flash firmware. Why it fights ZFS: OpenZFS expects a normal random-access block device. Copy-on-write, metadata updates, and resilvers scatter writes. A host-managed zoned disk will not behave like a regular drive unless the filesystem speaks the zone protocol — and OpenZFS / TrueNAS do not.

RAIDZ2 + zoned / host-managed SMR? No.

Do not put host-managed zoned devices in a TrueNAS / OpenZFS pool. They need special zone-aware software; ZFS is not that software today. Community guidance is blunt: HM-SMR “will not work with TrueNAS or ZFS” (TrueNAS feature request — not accepted).

The sneakier cousin: device-managed SMR

Some drives are SMR but hide it. Firmware presents a fake “normal” disk (device-managed SMR). The OS thinks it can write anywhere; under the hood the drive rewrites whole zones. Quiet day-to-day use can look fine — until a resilver or heavy write empties the drive’s small CMR cache and throughput collapses.

ServeTheHome measured this on a WD Red 4 TB SMR model vs CMR peers in a FreeNAS RAIDZ rebuild scenario: SMR is a poor match for the workload your RAIDZ2 pool will hit when a disk dies. iXsystems / TrueNAS advice matches the measurement: avoid SMR for ZFS.

Buy this instead

CMR (conventional magnetic recording) — also called PMR. Tracks sit side-by-side; random writes stay predictable. Check the exact model number on the datasheet before you pay. Marketing words like “NAS” or “Red” are not enough (the WD Red SMR episode taught that lesson industry-wide).

SATA vs SAS: which cable for a home NAS?

SATA and SAS are interfaces — how the drive talks to the motherboard or HBA — not “better RAID.” Both can carry CMR disks. For your mission (bulk media, photos, documents on a home box), the TrueNAS ZFS Primer framing still holds: ZFS wants commodity disks it controls directly, not a hardware RAID card doing clever things.

Course default

CMR SATA “NAS” drives for the RAIDZ2 data vdev. Skip SAS unless you already have a SAS backplane/HBA and a clear reason (cheap used enterprise CMR stock you verified, dense chassis that only speaks SAS, etc.). Never buy “zoned” / host-managed SMR / ZNS for this pool. If you need more ports than the board has, use an HBA in IT / JBOD mode so ZFS still sees each disk — not a hardware RAID volume (Primer).

One-line shopping checklist

  1. Layout from Lesson 5 locked (e.g. RAIDZ2 × 6–8).
  2. Same capacity CMR disks; verify CMR on the model’s datasheet.
  3. SATA interface unless your chassis forces SAS.
  4. No hardware RAID — passthrough / IT mode only.
  5. Still plan the 3-2-1 off-site copy from Lesson 4.
