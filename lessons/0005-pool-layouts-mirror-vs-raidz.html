Pool layouts: mirror vs RAIDZ

You know redundancy is not a backup. Now the decision that locks in for years: how the disks inside your NAS pool are grouped. Wrong layout means destroy-and-rebuild later. Right layout means capacity, fault tolerance, and performance that match your mission (photos, documents, a media library).

~14 min Concept Win: pick mirror vs RAIDZ2 for your workload, and a sensible drive count

Pool → vdevs → disks

A ZFS pool is built from one or more vdevs. Each vdev is a group of disks with a chosen topology (mirror, RAIDZ, …). The pool stripes data across its vdevs. Critical rule from the TrueNAS ZFS Primer:

Lose a whole vdev → lose the pool

Redundancy lives inside each vdev, not as a magic pool-wide safety net. A six-disk RAIDZ2 vdev can lose two disks. Add a single non-redundant disk as a new vdev “for more space,” lose that one disk, and the entire pool is gone. Never do that.

Topology is chosen when you create the vdev. You can often add another vdev later, or replace disks with larger ones, but you cannot casually turn a mirror into RAIDZ2. Plan before you click Create (RAIDZ vs Mirrors — home NAS).

The TrueNAS ZFS Storage Pool Layout white paper puts the same trade-off formally: for an N-wide RAIDZ with parity level p, space efficiency is (N−p)/N, and random IOPS stay roughly those of a single drive per vdev. Streaming throughput scales with (N−p). Mirrors trade capacity for IOPS that grow as you add pairs.

What your mission should lean toward

Your success criteria: photos, documents, and a media library on hardware you control. That workload is mostly large sequential reads/writes — not a hypervisor full of VMs.

Default recommendation for this course

One RAIDZ2 vdev, about 6–8 disks wide. Survive two failures, keep ~⅔–¾ of raw capacity, excellent for bulk media. Prefer mirrors only if the NAS’s main job becomes VMs / databases / heavy random IOPS (home NAS guide; Primer: mirrors favoured for large uncacheable random read loads).

Why not RAIDZ1?

RAIDZ1 survives only one disk failure. While a dead disk is being resilvered, every remaining disk is under heavy read load. On multi-terabyte drives that window is long — a second failure (or unrecoverable read error) during rebuild can kill the vdev. The Primer explicitly warns against RAIDZ1 for drives over about 1 TiB; modern home drives are far larger. For sovereignty-grade data: start at RAIDZ2.

Why not a 12-wide monster?

Wider RAIDZ2 looks efficient on paper. Resilver time stretches into days, IOPS stay ~one disk, and the blast radius of “something goes wrong mid-rebuild” grows. TrueNAS guidance: prefer roughly 3–9 disks per vdev; beyond that, add another vdev of the same type rather than one ultra-wide stripe (ZFS Primer — Disk number).

Four-bay reality check

If you only have four bays: RAIDZ2 × 4 is honest (50% usable, two-disk survival). Two 2-way mirrors also use 50% but favour IOPS. RAIDZ1 × 4 buys capacity at the cost of a single-failure budget — only if you already treat 3-2-1 backups as non-negotiable (you should anyway).

Still not a backup

RAIDZ2 that survives two dead drives still fails the fire / ransomware / rm -rf tests from Lesson 4. Layout chooses how redundancy works. 3-2-1 still chooses whether you can recover.
